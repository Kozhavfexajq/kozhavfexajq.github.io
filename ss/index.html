6 Need-to-know Things about Ancient China

#1: Location and Geography: The Yellow River Valley

Back in the old days (nearly 5000 years ago), China was first founded along the Yellow River valley, named for the yellow soil around the river, feeding into the yellow sea, and the Pacific Ocean. To the north are the Da and Xiao Hinggan Ling Mountains, as well as the Gobi Desert. To the west is the Takla Makan desert, more mountains, the plateau of Tibet, and the Himalayas. To the south are more hills and rivers, to which the Chinese moved to around 500 C.E., where the golden age started. If you go farther north, you reach the Siberian tundra, west is Central Asia, AKA, deserts and mountains, and to the south is India and Southeast Asia. If you go a bit farther East, you would reach Korea and Japan.

#2: Religion: The 3 Main Philosophies

Ancient China had 3 main philosophies, with Buddhism entering China around 200 B.C.E. First main philosophy: Confucianism: Basically, be a good person, and try to learn as much as possible. The main pros are that it would mean a society of nice people, trying to evolve their understanding as much as possible, but the cons are that people wouldn’t ever end up following it, as greed and arrogance are only human. Second man philosophy: Taoism: Basically, there are always balancing forces, yin and yang, people should be one with nature, rules and government are unimportant. Pros are that people would be more focused on nature, and peace in the world, cons are that government and rules would be neglected, leading people to do bad things. The third main philosophy was legalism. It basically means laws all the way, society should have harsh laws and punishments. Pros are that people would stop disobeying the law, cons are that if something disobeys the law, but it is morally the correct move, then the legal way would be chosen. Also, depending on how harsh the laws are, they could be rebelled against. They also worshipped ancestors, not directly in any of these philosophies. The older the ancestors, the more powerful they are. Royal ancestors were seen as intermediaries between the king/emperor and the high god (Shandgi). They also believed in a dual soul, the yin, corporeal soul, and the yang, ethereal soul. This was primarily involved in Taoism.

#3: Creations and Inventions: Technology

The main achievements of ancient China were inventions. Examples: silk, coined money, paper, wood making, porcelain, etc. Other inventions made later were gunpowder, compass, printing press, stronger bridges, canal locks, mechanical clocks, umbrellas, steel, earthquake detection, etc. All of these inventions helped China get a monopoly on a lot of trading. They would export their inventions, in return for the raw products they need, such as grapevine and cotton. The main building achievement was the great wall of China. Giant wall that took 2000 years to build, degrading, kind of useless. They also invented certain techniques, such as acupuncture, terrace farming, and apothecary herbal medicine.

#4: Politics and Rulers: The Dynastic Cycle

Ever since about 2000 BCE, until just over 100 years ago, there was a cycle of dynasties. A dynasty is founded by a powerful leader, they get the mandate of heaven, there is a period of peace and prosperity, then there is decline, then they lose the mandate of heaven and the people rebel, and then a new leader rises. It went from Xia, to Shang, to Zhou, to Qin, to Han, then a period where there were 3, then Jin, then another several at the same time, then Sui, then Tang, then another period of chaos, then Song, then Liao, then Xia, then Jin, then Yuan (Mongols), then Ming, then Qing. In the Shang dynasty, they had the first writing in that area, as well as bronze metallurgy. They also invented the Chinese calendar. In the Qin dynasty, there was this tyrannical leader named Qin Shi Huang, who expanded the empire greatly. It was the shortest lasting dynasty. During the Han, Tang, and Song dynasties, there was a period of great inventions, often referred to as the golden age of China.

#5: Money and Trade: Unfair Advantages

Most of their exports were finished goods that they invented, such as gunpowder and silk, as well as things like iron and cattle. They would import raw materials and resources, such as cotton, as well as fancy things like lapis lazuli and pearl. They would trade with almost everywhere, from India to Mesopotamia, to Egypt, and even Rome in later dynasties. They had the first coined money around 200 B.C.E., and the first paper (silk paper) money around 900 C.E.

#6: Society: Structure of Socials

At the top of the chain is the Emperor, followed by the government officials. Then, the peasants, then the artisans, then the merchants, then the enslaved people. You were born into your social class, but you could move up or down the stairs, hence the image of a stairlike social structure. Around the Han, Tang and Song dynasties, the Golden Age of China, they had a new social structure, the non-stair chart: The Emperor is at the top, controlling the Court, the civil servants. They control the Gentry which are above the peasants, who are above the merchants. However, the merchants have control over the purchase of land and education, and the peasants over the schooling and government service, both of which control the gentry, giving a balanced social structure.
